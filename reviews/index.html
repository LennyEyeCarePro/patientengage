The problem

Every manual review system fails.
Just at different speeds.

QR codes. Checkout asks. Signs on the counter. Manual texts. Each one works briefly and then stops. The result is always the same.

Manual ask
The verbal ask at checkout
Staff remember on slow days and forget on busy ones. Patients nod and move on. The ask depends on the right person, in the right mood, at the right moment. That combination rarely aligns.
Passive signage
QR codes and review us signs
A sign on the counter or a QR code on the receipt requires the patient to notice it, care enough to act, and follow through after they leave. Virtually no one does. The sign becomes wallpaper within a week.
Manual follow-up
Texting or emailing patients yourself
Some practices try to send review requests manually. It works for a week, then stops when the person responsible gets busy. There is no system. There is no consistency. The reviews stop coming and no one notices until the damage is done.
The consequence
Patients apply a recency filter.
Studies consistently show that the majority of patients only trust reviews written in the last 30 days. A 4.8 star rating with a last review from 14 months ago does not signal excellence. It signals: this practice used to be good. Something changed.
The Map Pack drop
Google rewards velocity, not history.
Google's local search algorithm weights recent review activity in Map Pack rankings. A competitor generating 5 reviews a month will outrank your historical 4.8 stars if you are generating zero. The practice does not disappear overnight. It decays slowly and invisibly until one day it is on page 2.
